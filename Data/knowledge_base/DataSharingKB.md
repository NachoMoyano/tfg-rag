## Pricing schemes

In this section we define the different pricing schemes usually found in data products.

### Subscription-based data products

**Subscription-based data products** are **data-driven services or datasets that users access by paying a recurring fee (monthly, yearly, etc.)** instead of buying them once. The subscription gives continuous access to **updated data, analytics, or insights**. They are common in **data platforms, APIs, and analytics tools** where the value comes from **fresh data and ongoing updates** rather than a static dataset.

Overall, subscription-based data products benefit providers because they create predictable recurring revenue and encourage long-term relationships with customers. For users, they provide reliable access to up-to-date data without the need to manage or maintain the datasets themselves.

### One-off data products

**One-off data products** are datasets or data-driven outputs that are **sold or delivered once for a single payment** , rather than through a recurring subscription. After the purchase, the customer typically receives the dataset, report, or analysis as a **static product** , and the transaction ends unless the customer decides to buy another update later.

In this model, the value of the product lies in the **specific dataset or analysis delivered at a particular moment in time** , rather than in continuous updates. The provider collects, processes, and packages the data, and then delivers it as a finished product. Unlike subscription-based data services, the provider usually has **no obligation to maintain or update the dataset** after the delivery.

Overall, one-off data products are typically easier to sell and purchase because they involve a single transaction. However, they generate **less predictable revenue for the provider** and may require repeated sales efforts. For customers, they are useful when the need for data is **temporary, project-based, or focused on a specific question at a given time**.

### Volume-based data products

**Volume-based data products** are data services in which the **price depends on the amount of data consumed or the number of requests made** . Instead of paying a fixed subscription fee or buying a dataset once, customers pay according to how much they use the data. This model is often described as **pay-as-you-go** pricing.

In this approach, the data provider charges based on measurable units such as the **number of API calls, records downloaded, queries executed, or gigabytes of data transferred** . The more data a customer consumes, the higher the total cost. This pricing structure is common when data is delivered through online platforms or APIs and usage can be tracked automatically.

Volume-based data products are attractive for customers because they allow organizations to **pay only for the data they actually use** , which can reduce costs when usage is low or unpredictable. For providers, this model aligns revenue with usage and can scale effectively as customers build applications that rely more heavily on the data service.

## Data Delivery Methods

In this section we provide information about different methods that data marketplaces and providers use to deliver data to data consumers.

### Amazon S3 buckets

**S3 buckets** are containers used to store data in ****Amazon Simple Storage Service** (Amazon S3)** , a cloud object storage service provided by **Amazon Web Services** (AWS). A bucket acts like a top-level folder in the cloud where files and datasets—called **objects** —are stored.

In Amazon S3, every piece of data is stored as an **object inside a bucket** . Each object typically consists of the file itself (such as an image, video, document, or dataset), metadata describing the file, and a unique identifier called a key. Buckets therefore serve as the organizational unit that groups related objects together.

Many data products sold by AWS Data Markeplace are delivered as S3 buckets. Other cloud service providers provide similar services like **Google Cloud Storage**, or **Microsoft Azure Storage**.

Buckets are globally unique across AWS, meaning that each bucket must have a unique name across all users of the platform. They are also created in a specific geographic region, which determines where the data is physically stored and can influence factors such as latency, cost, and regulatory compliance.

S3 buckets are commonly used for many types of data storage tasks. Organizations use them to store application assets such as images or videos, to maintain backups and archives, to host static websites, and to store large datasets used in analytics or machine learning pipelines. Because Amazon S3 is designed for high durability and scalability, a single bucket can store **virtually unlimited numbers of objects** .

Another important feature of S3 buckets is **access control** . Bucket owners can define permissions that determine who can read, write, or manage the objects stored inside them. Access can be configured for private use, shared within an organization, or even made publicly accessible for open datasets or website hosting.

In simple terms, an S3 bucket can be thought of as **a cloud-based storage container where files and data are stored and organized within Amazon’s infrastructure**.

### REST API

A REST API (Representational State Transfer Application Programming Interface) is a web-based interface that allows applications to communicate over the internet using a set of standardized rules and methods. REST APIs use HTTP protocols and standard operations like GET, POST, PUT, and DELETE to access and manipulate resources identified by URLs, supporting scalable, stateless, and flexible data exchange between systems.

REST APIs are widely used in web services and cloud platforms. For instance, developers interact with services such as **Amazon Simple Storage Service** from **Amazon Web Services** or the **Google Maps Platform** through REST APIs. Applications send HTTP requests to these services and receive responses, often formatted as JSON or XML.

One of the main advantages of REST APIs is their **simplicity and scalability** . Because they rely on standard web technologies, they can be used by many different programming languages and platforms. They are also stateless, meaning that each request from a client contains all the information needed for the server to process it, which simplifies system design and supports large-scale distributed systems.

In essence, a REST API is a **standardized way for applications to request and exchange data over the web** , enabling services, platforms, and applications to interact with each other efficiently.

### Streaming APIs

**Streaming APIs** are application programming interfaces that allow applications to receive **continuous, real-time streams of data** instead of making repeated requests to retrieve discrete pieces of information. In a streaming API, once a client establishes a connection with the server, the server continuously sends new data as it becomes available.

This model differs from traditional request–response APIs, such as **REST APIs** , where a client must send a new request each time it wants updated data. With a streaming API, the connection typically remains open, and the server pushes updates to the client whenever new events occur.

Streaming APIs are especially useful when applications need **real-time information** . For example, social media platforms have used streaming APIs to provide live updates of posts or events. They are common in domains such as financial markets, sensor networks, and Internet of Things (IoT) systems. Financial platforms, for instance, can stream continuously updated price information so that trading applications receive market data instantly.

Technically, streaming APIs can be implemented using several mechanisms, including **WebSockets, server-sent events (SSE), or long-lived HTTP connections** . These approaches allow the server to push new data to clients without requiring the client to repeatedly poll the server.

In practice, streaming APIs are particularly valuable when applications need **low latency and continuous updates** , such as monitoring systems, real-time analytics dashboards, live notifications, or event-driven architectures.

### E-mail

**data can be delivered through e-mail** , although this method is typically used only for **small datasets or periodic reports** rather than large-scale or real-time data delivery.

In this approach, the data provider sends the data directly to the recipient’s email address, usually as an **attachment** or as a **download link** included in the message. Common attachment formats include spreadsheets (such as CSV or Excel files), PDFs containing reports, or compressed archives containing multiple files.

Email delivery is often used for **scheduled reports or low-frequency data updates** . For example, a company might send a weekly analytics report generated from platforms such as **Tableau** or **Power BI** from **Microsoft**. In these cases, automated systems generate the report and distribute it by email to stakeholders at regular intervals.

Another situation where email is used is in **one-off data deliveries** . A consulting firm or data provider might send a dataset requested by a client as a downloadable file via email after completing an analysis or data extraction.

However, email is **not suitable for large or frequently updated datasets** . Email systems typically limit attachment sizes, often to around 20–25 MB. In addition, email delivery is inefficient for automated systems that require continuous data access or integration. For these reasons, larger or more dynamic data products are usually delivered through APIs, cloud storage services such as **Amazon Simple Storage Service** from **Amazon Web Services**, or data platforms.

In summary, email can be used as a **simple distribution channel for small datasets, reports, or occasional data deliveries** , but it is generally not appropriate for high-volume, real-time, or programmatically integrated data services.

### Feed API

A **feed API** is an application programming interface that provides **a continuously updated stream or regularly refreshed list of data items** , typically delivered in chronological order. Applications use a feed API to retrieve the latest entries from a data source, such as news articles, social media posts, events, or system updates.

In practice, a feed API exposes an endpoint that returns a **structured list of recent items** , often in formats such as JSON or XML. Each item in the feed usually contains fields such as a timestamp, an identifier, and content or metadata. When a client requests the feed, it receives the latest items and can display or process them in its own application.

Unlike streaming APIs, which push data continuously through an open connection, feed APIs are usually **queried periodically** . An application may request the feed every few minutes and receive the newest items added since the last request. Feed APIs are commonly used in **content distribution and notification systems**.

Feed APIs are conceptually related to web syndication technologies such as RSS feeds, which are widely used by content platforms like **WordPress** to distribute updates from blogs or websites.

In summary, a feed API provides a **structured, regularly updated list of new content or events** , allowing applications to stay synchronized with the latest information from a service.

### Export from a user interface

Some data products make data available through a user interface or dashboard that allows to filter and tailor data and then export the data to the end user.

In this delivery model, the platform typically provides controls that allow users to **filter, search, or configure the data before downloading it** . For example, a user might choose a date range, select specific variables, or apply filters to narrow down the results. After configuring the query, the user can export the resulting dataset in formats such as **CSV, Excel, or JSON** .

This approach is common in **analytics platforms and data portals** . Tools such as **Tableau** or **Power BI** from **Microsoft** often allow users to export the data behind a visualization or dashboard. Similarly, many statistical platforms, such as **Statista**, allow users to download datasets or charts directly from the website interface.

Delivering data through UI exports is particularly useful for **non-technical users** who may not be comfortable working with APIs or programming tools. It allows analysts, managers, or students to quickly obtain data for tasks such as reporting, presentations, or exploratory analysis.

However, this delivery method has several limitations. Because the process is manual, it is **not well suited for automated workflows or frequent updates** . If a dataset needs to be refreshed regularly, users must repeatedly log in, run the query, and export the file. Additionally, UI exports may impose limits on the amount of data that can be downloaded at once.

Overall, exporting data from a user interface is a **simple and accessible way to distribute datasets** , especially for occasional use or exploratory analysis, but it is less efficient than APIs or data pipelines when data needs to be integrated automatically into software systems or updated continuously.

### Data services in virtual machines

Delivering data through **services in virtual machines (VMs)** involves providing access to datasets or data-processing capabilities by hosting them on a virtualized server environment. In this model, the data or the software that serves the data runs inside a VM, which is a software-based emulation of a physical computer. Users or client applications connect to the VM to retrieve, query, or process the data.

A VM can host a variety of **data services** , such as databases, file servers, APIs, or analytics tools. For example, an organization might run a relational database (like MySQL or PostgreSQL) or a data warehouse inside a VM. Authorized users can then connect to that VM over the network, run queries, or download datasets. Similarly, a VM could host a REST or streaming API, allowing programmatic access to continuously updated data.

This approach is common in enterprise environments where organizations want **control over infrastructure, security, and customization** . Using VMs allows administrators to configure the operating system, install specific software, enforce access controls, and manage network settings. Providers like **Amazon Web Services** (AWS), **Microsoft Azure**, and **Google Cloud** all offer virtual machine services where customers can deploy data services securely.

Delivering data via VMs can be done in multiple ways. Some organizations allow **direct access to databases** , enabling users to query tables or download data. Others host **file servers or shared directories** inside the VM, from which users can copy datasets. In more advanced setups, VMs run **custom applications or analytics platforms** that expose data through dashboards, APIs, or batch export jobs.

The main advantages of this delivery method are **control, flexibility, and isolation** . Users can configure the VM exactly to their needs, and sensitive data can remain inside a controlled environment. However, there are trade-offs: setting up and maintaining VMs can require significant technical expertise, network configuration must be managed carefully, and scaling to many users may require additional infrastructure.

In short, delivering data through services in virtual machines is a **flexible, controlled approach** that allows organizations to host databases, APIs, or analytics services in an environment they fully manage, giving users networked access to data while keeping infrastructure and security under the organization’s control.

### SQL Queries

In this model, the provider hosts a **database or data warehouse** and allows users to access it using **Structured Query Language (SQL)** , the standard language for querying relational databases. Instead of downloading static files, users can **run custom queries** to retrieve exactly the data they need.

This method is commonly used with **cloud-hosted databases and data warehouses** such as **Snowflake**, **Google BigQuery**, or **Amazon Redshift**. Users connect to these systems via a client application, a web interface, or programmatically through SQL clients or APIs. They can filter, aggregate, join, and analyze large datasets in place, without needing to download the entire dataset locally.

Providing access via SQL queries is particularly valuable for **large or complex datasets** , where downloading everything would be inefficient or impractical. For example, a company might allow analysts to query historical sales data, user activity logs, or sensor readings directly in the database. This also supports **dynamic and ad hoc analysis** , since users can write queries tailored to their specific needs rather than relying on pre-generated reports.

Security and access control are critical in this setup. Providers can define **roles, permissions, and query limits** to ensure users access only the data they are authorized to see. Many platforms also support **query caching and cost controls** to manage performance and expenses in environments where users run large or frequent queries.

In essence, delivering data through SQL queries allows for **flexible, precise, and efficient access** to relational or structured datasets, making it a preferred option for analysts, data scientists, and applications that need to work with large-scale data interactively.

### Other methods

Beyond these methods there are several other ways data can be delivered, depending on the use case, scale, and technical sophistication of the users. Here’s a comprehensive overview in sentence form:

**Content delivery networks (CDNs)** can be used for delivering data that needs to be highly accessible and geographically distributed, such as software updates, large static datasets, or public datasets. CDNs cache data at multiple edge locations, reducing latency and improving download performance for users across the globe.

**Data lakes or warehouses** hosted on cloud platforms are another delivery mechanism. Organizations can give users access to structured or semi-structured datasets stored in platforms like **Snowflake**, **BigQuery**, or AWS Redshift. Users can query the data directly using SQL or analytics tools without needing to download the full dataset, which is efficient for very large datasets.

**Physical media delivery** is rare but still used for extremely large datasets where transferring over the internet is impractical. Providers may ship hard drives or SSDs containing data directly to the customer. This method is sometimes called “sneakernet” and can be used for petabyte-scale datasets.

**Streaming platforms** are also a type of delivery for continuous, real-time data. Beyond streaming APIs, organizations can use platforms like **Apache Kafka** or **AWS Kinesis** to deliver high-throughput data streams to subscribers or downstream applications for analytics, monitoring, or event-driven processing.

**Push notifications and webhooks** allow data to be delivered by sending updates automatically to a client system whenever a certain event occurs. Instead of the client repeatedly checking for updates, the provider pushes the data, often in small, event-focused payloads.

Finally, **hybrid approaches** combine methods—for instance, a platform might provide a subscription to a dataset that can be accessed via a feed API, with periodic UI exports available for non-technical users, or use a cloud-hosted database alongside a streaming service for real-time events.

In essence, the choice of data delivery method depends on factors like **dataset size, update frequency, technical expertise of users, integration needs, and cost considerations** . Modern systems often offer multiple delivery options to serve different user needs efficiently.

## Formats

In this section we will define and evaluate the different formats data can be delivered.

### SQL

**Data in SQL (.sql) format** refers to a file that contains **SQL statements used to create, populate, or modify a database** . Rather than being a dataset in a spreadsheet or CSV, it’s a text file that describes **how the data and database structure should be recreated** in a relational database.

Typically, a `.sql` file contains commands such as **`CREATE TABLE`** , **`INSERT INTO`** , **`UPDATE`** , or **`ALTER TABLE`** . For example, it might define a table’s columns, data types, and constraints, and then include statements to insert all the records into that table. Essentially, the file is a **script that can rebuild the database or its tables** when executed in a SQL database system like MySQL, PostgreSQL, or SQLite.

Using data in `.sql` format is common when sharing **relational datasets** because it allows the recipient to **fully recreate the database locally** . Instead of receiving just raw data, users receive both the **data and the schema** (the structure of tables, relationships, and constraints). This is particularly useful for developers, analysts, or organizations that want to **load the dataset directly into a database** without manually creating tables or formatting data.

For example, a provider might deliver a `.sql` file containing historical sales data. When the user runs the script in their database, it automatically creates the required tables and inserts all the sales records. This is more convenient for large datasets or datasets with complex relationships than downloading CSVs and importing them manually.

In short, a `.sql` file is **both a blueprint and a container for relational data** , allowing databases to be recreated exactly as intended, including the data itself, table structures, and sometimes even indexes or constraints.

### Text file format

**Data in text (.txt) file format** refers to information stored as **plain, unformatted text** in a simple file that can be opened and read by almost any text editor or program. Unlike specialized formats such as `.csv`, `.json`, or `.sql`, a `.txt` file does not enforce a particular structure, schema, or data type; it simply contains characters, lines, and possibly delimiters that represent the data.

Text files are extremely flexible and can contain **lists, tables, logs, notes, or any kind of textual information** . Each line is usually treated as a separate record, and delimiters such as spaces, commas, or tabs can be used to separate fields if the data is structured. For example, a `.txt` file might contain one word or number per line, or it could represent a table where each line contains multiple values separated by commas or tabs.

The main advantage of `.txt` files is **universality** —they can be opened and edited with basic tools like Notepad, VS Code, or terminal editors on any operating system. They are also **lightweight** , easy to share, and ideal for **simple data exports or logs** .

However, text files have some limitations. Since they don’t enforce a schema or data types, it’s up to the user or program reading the file to **interpret the contents correctly** . Large datasets or complex relational data are harder to manage in plain text compared to structured formats like SQL, CSV, or JSON.

In short, a `.txt` file is a **plain text container for data** , highly flexible and easy to use, but typically better suited for **simple, small, or human-readable datasets** rather than complex, structured, or large-scale data.

### CSV format

**Data products delivered in CSV format** are datasets provided as **Comma-Separated Values (CSV) files** , which is one of the most common ways to share structured data. In a CSV file, each row represents a single record (such as a person, transaction, or event), and each column represents a field or attribute (such as name, date, or value). Columns are separated by commas (or sometimes other delimiters like semicolons), making the data **plain text but structured** so it can be easily read by software.

CSV is widely used because it is **simple, lightweight, and compatible with almost any application** . Spreadsheets like Microsoft Excel or Google Sheets, statistical tools like R or Python’s pandas library, and databases can all read and import CSV files easily. This makes it an ideal format for delivering data products to analysts, researchers, or businesses that need to process or visualize the data.

Data products in CSV format are often used for **one-off deliveries, exports from dashboards, or downloadable datasets from data platforms** . For example, a company like **Statista** might allow users to download statistical datasets in CSV format, or a sales platform might export transaction data from its dashboard as a CSV file for analysis.

The main advantages of CSV-based data products are **universality, human readability, and simplicity** , while the limitations include the lack of **support for complex relationships, hierarchical data, or strict data types** . For very large datasets, CSV files can also become cumbersome, since they are stored as plain text and may require significant memory to process.

In summary, delivering data products in CSV format provides a **structured, accessible, and easy-to-use representation of data** , ideal for analysis, reporting, and integration into a wide range of tools.

### Spreadsheet format (.xlsx)

**Data products delivered in spreadsheet format (such as `.xlsx`)** are datasets provided as **Excel workbooks** or similar spreadsheet files. Unlike plain CSV files, spreadsheet files can store **structured data along with formatting, formulas, multiple sheets, charts, and metadata** , making them more versatile for analysis and reporting.

In an `.xlsx` file, each sheet represents a table of data, where rows are records and columns are fields or variables. Spreadsheet features allow data providers to include **pre-calculated metrics, summaries, pivot tables, or visualizations** directly within the file, which can make it easier for end-users to explore the data without additional processing.

These products are commonly used for **business reports, statistical datasets, financial data, or survey results** . For example, a company like **Statista** might allow users to download a market research dataset as an Excel file, with multiple sheets for different regions or categories, including charts and aggregated statistics. Similarly, a sales platform could export transaction data with formulas and summaries built in.

The main advantages of spreadsheet-based data products are **usability, immediate readability, and built-in analytical features** , making them ideal for **non-technical users or teams performing ad hoc analysis** . Users can open the files in Microsoft Excel, Google Sheets, or LibreOffice and start working with the data immediately.

However, spreadsheets are not ideal for **very large datasets or automated workflows** , because they are limited by file size, memory constraints, and lack of programmatic access compared to database or API-based delivery.

In short, delivering data in spreadsheet format provides a **ready-to-use, human-friendly version of structured data** , often with added analytics or visualizations, which is particularly convenient for reporting, presentations, and manual analysis.

### JSON

**Data products delivered in JSON format** are datasets provided as **JavaScript Object Notation (JSON) files** , a lightweight and structured data format widely used for exchanging information between systems. JSON represents data as **nested objects and arrays** , allowing it to capture **complex, hierarchical, or relational structures** that cannot be easily represented in flat formats like CSV or spreadsheets.

In a JSON file, data is organized using **key-value pairs** , where each key identifies a field and the corresponding value can be a string, number, boolean, array, or another object. For example, a dataset of users might store each user as an object containing nested fields like name, email, and a list of orders. This structure makes JSON ideal for datasets that include **multi-level relationships or variable-length lists** .

JSON-based data products are commonly used in **web services, APIs, and software applications** . For instance, a platform providing real-time data through a REST API might allow users to download or stream the data in JSON format. Many cloud services and analytics tools, such as **Amazon S3**, **BigQuery**, or **Google Cloud**, often store or deliver data in JSON because it can be easily parsed programmatically and integrated into applications.

The advantages of JSON-based data products include **flexibility, readability for developers, and compatibility with modern programming languages** , making it suitable for automated workflows, web applications, or data pipelines. JSON files can represent complex datasets without losing structure, unlike CSV or plain text.

The limitations are that JSON is **less human-friendly for manual analysis** compared to spreadsheets, and very large JSON files can become memory-intensive to process unless streamed or parsed incrementally.

In short, data products delivered in JSON format provide a **structured, hierarchical, and programmatically accessible way to share data** , ideal for developers, applications, and automated systems that need to consume, process, or integrate data efficiently.

### XML

**Data products delivered in XML format** are datasets provided as **Extensible Markup Language (XML) files** , a structured, text-based format designed to store and transport data. XML represents data using **tags and nested elements** , which define both the **structure and meaning** of the information. Each piece of data is enclosed in a pair of tags, and hierarchical relationships can be expressed through nesting, making XML suitable for **complex or tree-like datasets** .

For example, a dataset of books might include each book as an `<book>` element with nested tags like `<title>`, `<author>`, and `<year>`. This structure allows XML to represent **multi-level relationships, metadata, and attributes** clearly, which is useful for systems that need both the data and its context.

XML-based data products are commonly used in **enterprise systems, web services, and legacy applications** . Many older APIs and B2B systems still deliver data in XML because it is **platform-independent, self-describing, and supports validation** through XML Schema (XSD). For instance, government statistical agencies, financial services, or supply chain platforms might provide datasets as XML files for automated consumption by other systems.

The advantages of XML include its **flexibility, ability to represent hierarchical data, and support for metadata and validation** , which ensures the integrity of the data. It is also widely supported by programming languages and tools for parsing, transformation (e.g., XSLT), and integration.

However, XML files tend to be **larger and more verbose than formats like JSON or CSV** , which can make them less efficient for very large datasets or high-performance applications. They are also **less human-readable** compared to spreadsheets or simple text files.

In short, data products delivered in XML format provide a **structured, hierarchical, and self-describing way to share complex datasets** , making them suitable for enterprise systems, automated data exchange, and environments where strict validation and metadata are important.

### Binary (.bin) format

**Data delivered in binary (.bin) format** is data provided as a **raw binary file** , which contains information in a machine-readable format rather than human-readable text. Unlike formats such as CSV, JSON, or XML, a `.bin` file stores data as **bytes** , which can represent numbers, text, images, audio, or any other type of information in a compact, encoded form.

Binary files are commonly used when **performance, file size, or precision** is critical. Because the data is stored in its raw binary representation, `.bin` files are usually **smaller and faster to read or write** compared to equivalent text-based formats. However, they are not easily readable without the appropriate program or library to interpret the structure of the data.

Data products delivered in `.bin` format are often found in **scientific computing, machine learning, image processing, or software distribution** . For example, a machine learning platform might provide trained models as `.bin` files, a satellite imagery provider might deliver compressed binary raster data, or a software company might distribute firmware or configuration data in `.bin` files.

The advantages of binary data include **compactness, speed, and support for precise numeric formats** , which are essential for large datasets or performance-sensitive applications. The main limitation is that **specialized software or code is needed to read, interpret, and convert the data** , making `.bin` less suitable for casual users or simple analysis.

In short, data delivered in binary format provides a **compact, efficient, and machine-friendly way to distribute large or complex datasets** , but it requires the appropriate tools or programs to access and use the data effectively.

### PDF or Word format

**data can be delivered as a report in PDF or Word format** , but this approach is usually intended for **human consumption rather than programmatic use** . In short, delivering data as PDF or Word reports is a **presentation-focused approach** , ideal for summaries, insights, and reporting, but not suitable when the goal is to perform automated analysis or integrate the data into software systems.

### Shapefile

**Data can be delivered as a shapefile** , which is a common format for **geospatial or geographic information system (GIS) data** . A shapefile stores **vector data** —points, lines, and polygons—that represent geographic features such as cities, roads, rivers, or administrative boundaries. It is widely used in mapping, spatial analysis, and location-based applications.

Technically, a shapefile is not a single file but a **collection of at least three mandatory files** with extensions like `.shp` (geometry), `.shx` (shape index), and `.dbf` (attribute data). Additional files like `.prj` (coordinate system) or `.cpg` (encoding) may also be included. Together, these files store both the **spatial information (geometry)** and the **attribute data** describing each geographic feature.

Shapefiles are commonly used in **urban planning, environmental analysis, transportation, and GIS research** . For example, a government agency might provide a shapefile of census tracts with population data, or a mapping company might deliver road network data in shapefile format for analysis in tools like **ArcGIS** or **QGIS**.

The advantages of shapefiles are that they are **widely supported by GIS software** , can store complex spatial relationships, and combine geographic coordinates with attribute data. The limitations include **file size restrictions, a flat attribute table without relational capabilities** , and that they are less suitable for very large datasets compared to modern spatial formats like GeoJSON or spatial databases.

In short, delivering data as a shapefile provides a **standardized way to share geospatial data** that can be visualized, analyzed, and integrated into GIS applications, making it ideal for geographic and spatial data products.

## Database format (.dbf)

**Database format (.dbf) for data products** refers to datasets delivered in the **dBASE database file format** , which is a legacy format originally used by the **dBASE database management system** . Each field has a defined **data type** such as numeric, text, date, or logical, which allows the database to enforce basic data integrity.

DBF files were widely used in early database and GIS systems, and today they are still supported by many tools, especially in **geospatial and statistical applications** . For example, shapefiles—a standard format for GIS data—store attribute tables in `.dbf` files, alongside `.shp` files for geometry and `.shx` files for the spatial index. The main advantages of DBF files are **simplicity, wide compatibility, and support for structured tabular data** , which makes them suitable for **small- to medium-sized datasets** . In practice, DBF is often used for **legacy data delivery, GIS attribute tables, and small-scale database exports** . It allows data providers to deliver structured tables that can be opened in older database software, spreadsheets, or GIS tools without requiring complex setup.

In short, a data product delivered in `.dbf` format provides **structured, tabular data in a widely compatible legacy database format** , often used for GIS attributes or simple flat-table datasets.

### Image data formats

## Update frequency

The **frequency with which data providers update their data** or **refresh rate** can vary widely depending on the nature of the data, the technical infrastructure, and the needs of users. In practice, data products are usually updated according to one of several typical **update frequencies** , ranging from real-time updates to very infrequent releases.

Some data products provide **real-time updates** , meaning that the data is refreshed immediately as new events occur. This is common in systems that monitor rapidly changing information such as financial market data, sensor networks, or social media streams. In these cases, the data is often delivered through streaming APIs or event-driven systems.

Other data products update data **near real-time** , where updates occur with very small delays, typically seconds or minutes. This approach is common for operational dashboards, transportation systems, or monitoring platforms where information must remain very current but does not require true real-time streaming.

Many data products are updated on a **regular schedule** , such as hourly, daily, weekly, or monthly. Daily updates are common for operational datasets like transaction records or website analytics. Weekly or monthly updates are typical for aggregated statistics, business reports, or market research data. For example, statistical platforms such as **Statista** often update datasets periodically as new reports become available.

Some data products are updated **quarterly or annually** , especially when the underlying data changes slowly. Examples include census statistics, economic indicators, or large research datasets published by government agencies or academic institutions.

Finally, certain data products may be **static or one-time releases** . These datasets are published once and rarely updated, such as historical archives or research datasets tied to a specific study.

In summary, the update frequency of data products can range from **real-time, near real-time, hourly, daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, or annual updates** , depending on how quickly the underlying data changes and how timely the information needs to be for users.

### Temporal Resolution

In 2026, time resolution is categorized into four distinct speeds, driven by the shift toward real-time AI and autonomous decision-making.---

1. Real-Time / Streaming (Milliseconds to Seconds). This is the highest level of temporal resolution. **Data is processed as an "event" the moment it happens.** Frequency range is 1ms to 30 seconds. High precision that allows to capture micro-fluctuations that disappear in averages. Use Cases include High-frequency trading, fraud detection, and self-driving car sensor arrays. In 2026, this is becoming the "baseline" for AI agents that need to react instantly to market changes.
2. Sub-Daily / "Intraday" (Minutes to Hours). Data is updated several times throughout the day, providing a detailed look at daily cycles. Frequency ranges minutely to hourly. Medium precision that captures "peaks and valleys," such as the morning rush hour or a sudden cloud passing over a solar farm. Use Cases include dynamic pricing for ride-sharing, electricity grid management, and "Same-Day" satellite tasking where a satellite captures an image and delivers it within hours.
3. Periodic / Sync (Daily to Weekly). This is the traditional "batch" processing level. **It provides a steady heartbeat of information but misses short-term spikes.** Frequency ranges from 24 hours to 7 days. Precision is excellent for identifying consistent trends and "state changes" (e.g., a building foundation being poured). Use Cases include retail inventory management, regional crop health monitoring (using satellites like Sentinel-2), and corporate "brain drain" tracking.
4. Low Temporal / Archival (Monthly to Yearly). This level is used for "big picture" historical analysis or slow-moving phenomena. Frequency can be Monthly, Quarterly, or Annually. Precision is lower and data is highly smoothed; all daily or hourly "noise" is removed. Use Cases include urban sprawl analysis, climate change modeling, and long-term economic forecasting.

### Temporal resolution vs refresh rate

While the terms are often used interchangeably in casual conversation, in the context of high-end data products, they describe two different parts of the data's journey: one is about **collection** and the other is about **availability**.

**Temporal resolution refers to the frequency at which the sensor or source actually captures a new data point.**^^ It is the "real-world" heartbeat of the information. For example, if a satellite passes over Madrid once every five days to take a picture, its temporal resolution is five days. This is a physical constraint of the data gathering process. You cannot see what happened on day three because the data simply does not exist.

Refresh rate, on the other hand, refers to how often the data product or user interface updates the information it displays to you. This is a digital delivery constraint. Using that same satellite example, if the service provider takes two days to process the image and then uploads it to your dashboard, the refresh rate of your dashboard might be weekly, even though the data itself was captured days earlier.

The gap between these two is often called latency. In a perfect data product, the refresh rate matches the temporal resolution, meaning as soon as a new data point is captured, it is immediately available to the user. However, in many corporate or Earth-observation products, you might have high temporal resolution (data captured every hour) but a low refresh rate (your report only updates once a day). This means you are often looking at "stale" data even if more recent data has technically been recorded.

Another way to distinguish them is by looking at "Sync" versus "Stream." In a streaming data product, the refresh rate is near-instant, pushing every change in temporal resolution to the user in milliseconds. In a synced product, the data sits in a database until a scheduled "refresh" pulls it into the visualization layer. Understanding this difference is critical for decision-making; if you are tracking a moving target like a wildfire or a fluctuating stock price, a high temporal resolution is useless if your refresh rate lags behind by several hours.

### On-demand data

Some data products provide data **on demand**. This means that **data is generated or retrieved only when a user or system requests it** , rather than being delivered automatically at fixed intervals or continuously streamed.

In this model, the user sends a **query or request** —for example through an API, a database query, or a user interface—and the system processes that request and returns the data immediately or shortly afterward. The data is therefore **produced dynamically at the time of the request** , based on the parameters specified by the user.

On-demand delivery is common in systems where users need **flexible access to specific subsets of data** . For instance, a user might request data for a particular geographic region, time range, or category. Instead of downloading an entire dataset, the system retrieves only the relevant information and returns it in response to the query.

This approach is widely used in **data platforms, analytics systems, and APIs** . For example, a user might request a custom dataset from a database or query a cloud analytics platform such as **Google BigQuery** to retrieve results only when the query is executed.

The main advantages of on-demand data delivery are **flexibility, efficiency, and reduced data transfer** , since users receive only the information they need at that moment. However, it may require **computational resources at query time** , and response times can vary depending on the complexity of the request and the size of the underlying dataset.

In short, a data product that provides **on-demand data** allows users to **request and retrieve specific data whenever they need it** , rather than receiving data through scheduled updates or continuous streams.

### Location data

At its simplest, location data is information that describes the physical position of an object or person on Earth at a specific point in time. It essentially turns the question "Where?" into a set of readable data points. In the context of data products, location data is rarely just a single point; it is a layer of context that connects digital activity to the physical world.

This data generally falls into two categories based on how it is measured. Absolute location refers to precise coordinates, usually expressed as latitude and longitude, such as **$40.7128^{\circ}\text{ N}, 74.0060^{\circ}\text{ W}$** for New York City. Relative location describes a position in relation to other known landmarks or boundaries, such as being within 50 meters of a specific store or inside a particular neighborhood.

The method of collection determines how accurate the data is. GPS provides high accuracy by using satellite signals, while Wi-Fi or cellular triangulation offers medium accuracy. IP addresses provide the lowest accuracy, usually only identifying a city or region, whereas short-range sensors like beacons can provide extremely high accuracy down to a few centimeters.

When you integrate location into a product, it usually serves one of three purposes: geofencing to trigger actions based on boundaries, spatial analysis to identify patterns like foot traffic heatmaps, or enrichment to add geographical context to other data points. Because location data can reveal personal habits, it is considered sensitive information. Data products must handle it carefully through anonymization or by intentionally lowering the granularity—such as sharing a general neighborhood instead of an exact street address—to protect user privacy.

### Tracking sessions of individuals

Some data products allow to track the sessions of anonymized individuals, and provide very granular information while safeguarding privacy of data subjects. For example, a location retail dataset may identify other merchants where individuals that entered a shop went. For example, datasets providing information about companies may identify people working in the company.

This is the core of privacy-preserving data analytics, where data products perform a sophisticated balancing act by providing granular, individual-level behavioral insights without ever exposing the actual identity of the person. In the retail and corporate examples you mentioned, this is usually achieved through a combination of high-tech mathematics and clever data architecture.

To track a session across different locations, such as knowing a person went from a clothing store to a coffee shop, the product typically uses a persistent pseudonym. Instead of using a name or a hardware ID, the system generates a random string of characters like a unique alphanumeric code. This allows the data product to show that a specific ID visited multiple merchants, giving the user the behavioral pattern without the provider ever knowing the human identity behind the movement.

To prevent someone from guessing an identity based on a unique path, these products often use a principle called k-anonymity. If only one person in a small town visited a luxury watch shop followed by a specific dive bar, it might be easy to deduce who they are. To safeguard privacy, the data product will only show a path or a segment if a minimum number of individuals, such as fifty or one hundred people, performed that same series of actions. If the group is too small to provide "safety in numbers," the data is suppressed or blurred to prevent re-identification.

A more advanced mathematical technique is differential privacy, which is used by companies like Apple and Google. The system intentionally adds a small amount of statistical noise to the dataset. This noise is calculated so that it does not change the overall trend, such as the percentage of shoppers who also visit a competitor, but it makes it mathematically impossible to be certain about the specific actions of any one individual in the set.

In the case of company datasets or firmographic data, the granularity usually shifts from the individual to the role or cohort. Instead of identifying a specific person, the product might report that a company has a certain number of senior engineers with specific skills. This allows for competitive intelligence, such as identifying "brain drain" when a cluster of people leaves a firm for a startup, or identifying "leakage" in retail when customers frequently visit a competitor’s bakery after shopping at your store. This level of detail remains a goldmine for strategic decision-making while staying within the bounds of data privacy laws.

### Ientifying companies

While identifying corporations in data products is generally more permissible than identifying individuals, it is not without significant legal, ethical, and strategic complications. In many jurisdictions, the rules of the game for corporate data are shifting away from the idea that anything goes as long as it isn't a person. One primary concern is the trade secret barrier. While a company’s physical location is public, its internal data—such as specific supply chain routes, the number of employees in a specialized R&D unit, or transaction volumes—can be considered proprietary trade secrets. If a data product reveals too much granular detail about these internal operations, it can face legal challenges for misappropriation. For instance, the EU Data Act, which became highly relevant in 2025 and 2026, creates a complex balance by encouraging data sharing while giving companies the right to protect metadata marked as confidential.

Another major issue involves anti-competitive behavior and antitrust risks. If a data product allows competitors to see exactly what each other is doing in real-time, it can inadvertently lead to algorithmic collusion. For example, if major airlines use a data product that identifies exactly when a competitor changes a route or a price, they might match each other's moves through automated systems, which ultimately harms the consumer. Consequently, large companies that control massive datasets about other businesses are often scrutinized by antitrust regulators, like the FTC or the European Commission, for maintaining an unfair competitive advantage that prevents smaller players from entering the market.

A common mistake in this field is assuming that because a dataset identifies a company, it does not involve personal data. This is often referred to as the "natural person" overlap or the GDPR trap. In many countries, data about a sole proprietorship or a one-person company is legally treated as personal data, meaning that identifying the business is the same as identifying the individual owner. Furthermore, if a dataset identifies a company and its specific roles in a small office—such as a single Senior Python Developer in a specific branch—the data product has effectively identified a private individual, triggering strict privacy laws like the GDPR or CCPA.

Reputational risks and corporate defamation also present a significant problem. Data products that provide insights into corporate behavior, such as environmental scores or financial health, risk legal action if their data is inaccurate. If a data product incorrectly identifies a company as being in financial distress or having high carbon emissions due to a data error, it can cause the company's stock to drop or its partners to flee. Unlike individuals, corporations have the resources to sue for commercial disparagement or libel. Finally, there is the issue of intellectual property and web scraping. As of 2026, many companies have implemented strict technical barriers and terms of service against AI and data scrapers. Identifying a company using data that was scraped in violation of a contract can lead to breach of contract lawsuits, even if the data itself is technically public. The current shift is toward data sovereignty, where companies increasingly view their hiring patterns and spending as assets they must control.

### Spatial resolution

**Spatial resolution in data products refers to the size of the smallest possible feature that can be detected or represented.** In 2026, the industry has standardized these into four main "buckets" based on their precision and use cases.

1. **Very High Resolution (VHR): Sub-Meter**. This is the most granular level, where a single pixel represents an area of 50 cm or less. These are high-end commercial products now reach as low as **5 cm to 15 cm** through aerial photography or advanced satellites like Maxar or Pléiades Neo. Data allows to distinguish individual vehicles, building details, light poles, and even specific items in outdoor inventory (like solar panels or cattle). Use Cases include urban planning, construction site monitoring, and high-stakes security.
2. **High Resolution (HR): 1 meter to 5 meters**. Data at this level covers a square of roughly **1 to 5 meters** per pixel. This is the standard for modern commercial mapping and high-quality environmental tracking. It allows to see road networks, large trees, individual buildings, and general infrastructure layouts. Use Cases include real estate analysis, forestry management, and detailed agricultural monitoring (crop health).
3. **Medium Resolution (MR): 5 meters to 30 meters**. This is the "workhorse" level of spatial data, often provided for free by government agencies like NASA (Landsat) and the ESA (Sentinel). The standard precision ranges are typically **10m, 20m, or 30m**. It allows to see major land-use patterns, city-wide boundaries, large water bodies, and significant deforestation. Use Cases include global climate monitoring, regional agricultural forecasting, and disaster response (e.g., tracking the spread of a wildfire).
4. Low/Coarse Resolution (LR): 30 meters to 1 kilometer+. Data at this level is used for broad, "big picture" analysis where detail is less important than total coverage. Precision often starts at **100m** and can go up to **1km** or more for atmospheric models. It allows to see Entire mountain ranges, ocean currents, and continental weather patterns. Use Cases include global meteorology, oceanography, and large-scale environmental studies.

**4.3** **How is data being priced?**
Figure 6a provides a summary of the most widely adoptedpricing mechanisms. Most pricing schemes are in line withthe current state-of-the-art [58]:
- Most buyers pay a lump-sum as a **fixed one-off** for a dataset,or a **fixed subscription** charge for accessing a stream or aservice for a period of time.
- **Customized** . Price (and the product) is personalized depending on who the buyer is and what the data is intended to beused for.
- **Open** . The platform lets buyers and sellers agree on theprices, and eventually consent to the data exchange. This isthe preferred approach of _enablers_ that focus on facilitatingdata exchanges but do not involve themselves in setting theireconomic terms.

- **Volume-based** . Price directly depends on the volume ofinformation that is downloaded or accessed (e.g., contacts,images), often with volume discounts.
- **Bid by buyer** . Buyers place bids (e.g., in a _PIMS_ ) that sellersmust accept for the transaction to take place. This avoidssellers deciding on an upfront asking price.
- **Usage-based** pricing is frequently used for API calls andoffered in tiers. Charges include volume discounts and dependon the number and type of calls.In addition, we identify some other interesting mechanismsbeing used in specific contexts.First, MyDex used to charge transactions using **revenue****sharing** : when a buyer purchased the rights to access a user’spersonal data, the platform claimed its rights to 4% of therevenues that such a buyer made on the platform from thatindividual. Digi.me, a _PIMS enabler_, also mentions this pricingscheme. Although innovative in terms of pricing data, its feasibility is still to be proven: would a _PIMS_ be able to control howmuch money buyers are making from each user and chargeaccordingly? None of them are using this scheme now.**Cost per 1,000 impressions**, **cost per click** and **percent-****age of gross media expenses** are specific to PMPs of onlineadvertising platforms (e.g., LiveRamp, Oracle or Kochava),thanks to their end-to-end control of online ad campaigns.Finally, **auctions** are very popular price setting mechanismsin other fields, and they are widely used in online advertisingwhere advertisers bid in real time to show their ads to a userbrowsing a certain webpage [57]. Nonetheless, they are not socommon when selling data, due to its non-rivalrous nature meaning selling a piece data to A does not prevent from sellingthe same data to B, hence A and B do not behave as rivals. Even though some works of research have already defined a wholefamily of auctions that artificially creates competition amonginterested buyers [34, 35], we found only one enabler (OceanProtocol) that mentions auctions as a potential mechanism toset the prices of data products.**4.4** **Who sets the price of data products?**
The answer to this question again depends on the businessmodel, as Fig, 6b shows. Whereas providers tightly controlthe price of their data or services, _PIMSs_ give more control totheir individual users (the actual data subjects), and usually letthem agree with buyers on transaction prices. Although DMsusually play an active role in setting the prices for data products on their platform, they always do it in conjunction withproviders. In fact, some of them (Dawex, Battlefin) charge foradvisory services in setting the prices for their data products.Such advisory services for pricing are empirically providedsince developing a more rigorous methodology for data pricingremains an open challenge ( _Challenge 2_ ).
**4.5** **How do entities deal with payments?**
Whereas data providers have traditionally been charged fortheir services in fiat money (dollars, euros, etc.), 55% of surveyed _PIMSs_ and almost 40% of marketplaces are using cryptocurrencies instead. The benefits they seek by using thisalternative include an increased speed of transfers, a higheravailability if compared to going through banks or establishments, and a greater liquidity. Real-time data exchanges likethe ones trading with IoT sensor data are broadly opting forcryptocurrencies when it comes to liquidate payments.
**4.6** **Do data trading platforms charge users****for accessing their services?**
DTEs that operate as platforms do not only charge users forthe data they consume, but for other concepts such as delivering data, or even just for gaining access to their services.Again, such additional platform charges vary greatly betweenbusiness models.

In general, PIMS are free for data subjects. On the one hand,this makes sense since they provide the platform with PI towork with, and also make the promise of increased privacy anddata protection more appealing. On the other hand, it raisesconcern over the profitability of users who are unwilling toshare their data and are using PIMS for such purposes. Data buyers, who are also usually welcome and free to join theplatform, often just pay for the data they acquire. In somecases, potential buyers are asked for a one-off connection feeor charged a periodic subscription (see Figure 7) to get accessto the platform. Finally, some PIMS demand details about thebuyer signing up to the platform to customize access charges.Conversely, charging buyers and especially data sellers foraccess is more usual in the case of DMs (see Figs. 8a and 8b),either through:
  - time-based subscriptions, often using a freemium model;
  - revenue sharing, where the platform keeps a percentage of the total sales;
  - one-off fees to connect to the system.
A few _niche_ _DMs_ (Otonomo) and most PMPs offer partnership models to big data sellers, an _ad_ _hoc_ agreement toshare data frequently used by DPs. Interestingly, a niche DM(Caruso) requires a partnership agreement to be signed bybuyers, which requires their participation as shareholders ifthey are willing to use the platform.Regarding PIMS and DM-enablers, they welcome full-fledgedPIMS to use their technology and usually charge pay-as-yougo IaaS/PaaS-like fees based on the number of API calls orthe volume of data they deliver. Some DMs (e.g., AWS orSnowflake) do charge data shipping fees to both parties, too.
**4.7** **How do entities trade data?**
Some specific characteristics of data, in particular its zero-costreplicability and its inherently combinatorial value, make thisattractive asset considerably more difficult to be priced andsafely traded [58, 65]. In economics, a good or service is called_excludable_ if it is possible to prevent consumers who havenot paid for it from having access to it. In addition, data is

non-depletable and hence a _non-rivalrous_ good: selling data toA does not prevent the owner from selling it again to B.Entities trading data aim at somehow making it _excludable_and therefore a club good. Indeed, this is a key challengein building a flourishing economy around data. This sectionprovides some additional insights about how entities in oursurvey are attempting to achieve this goal. In the followingsubsections, we answer questions regarding where entitiestake data from, what they provide buyers with, and how users
- both from the buy and sell sides - gain access to data.Since the conclusions are very different for DPs, DMs andPIMS, we present them separately in subsections.
_4.7.1_ _Data Providers._ As Fig. 9 shows, DPs leverage the internet and access to exclusive self-enriched data sources toprovide buyers with access to data either through APIs or bulkdownloads, and preferably through web-services or specificapplications. Note that they are not meant to be two-sidedplatforms, but players oriented to provide their data or theirdata-driven services to their customers. Should they requireproprietary information from third parties, they establish partnerships or bilateral agreements to access such exclusive information, which they eventually enrich and resell. Therefore,DPs control the whole go-to-market process, and conceal theidentity of their partners and the sources of their information,unless disclosing them adds any value (e.g., credibility) to theirbusiness and hence helps with sales.As an exception, PMPs integrated in data-driven services(e.g., spatio-temporal data marketplaces integrated in GIScloud SPs) allow third-party DPs to sell data within their platform. Unlike DMs, PMPs carefully select authorized DPs, whooften sign private partnership agreements with them. Moreover, they deliver data to be used within their system or services, and only to their users, which is why such marketplacesqualify as _private_ .
_4.7.2_ _Data Marketplaces._ Figure 10 shows that DMs collectand sometimes enrich or combine data from different DPs(sellers), who have signed the DM’s public terms of use. Similarto DPs, data is often delivered to buyers as a bulk downloador through APIs. Although some of them restrict deliverymethods to get access to data through their platforms (e.g.,AWS marketplace offers access to data stored in Amazon S3services). they often resort to APIs and web services for usersto manage their transactions and data within the system.
_4.7.3_ _PIMS._ PIMS collect and manage personal data fromindividuals. PIMS help users retrieve their PI from third partieslike social networks or e-mail services, which they call their_data_ _providers_ . Users can share their personal informationwith the platform, as well, and all their personal data arestored in their devices. Then, PIMS may sell such personaldata to potential buyers, or either share it with third partiesupon the owners’ consent. Most frequently, PIMS users usea mobile application to get access to the service, which alsoallows them to manage their consent to share their PI andmonitor data transactions. Most of them provide buyers with APIs or web services to gain access to data. As opposed toDPs and DMs, some PIMS ask entities willing to gain accessto acquired data to integrate their apps and systems (MyDex,GeoDB, DataWallet).PIMS deliver data in technologically innovative ways. Forexample, some of them provide access to encrypted data streamsby sending temporary keys that are revoked once the subscription expires. Some of them resort to hashed temporary URLsto provide buyers with access to data for a certain period.Some PIMS still do not provide an automated platform forbuyers to get data and results, but instead they negotiate directly with buyers, and generate the data to be shared withdata buyers case by case.**4.8** **How do entities store data products?**
_PIMSs_ usually opt for a more decentralized architecture byleveraging data subjects or providers to store and processusers’ data. With some exceptions, they avoid making copiesof personal information, which is retrieved from the users’personal data storage, instead. On the contrary, DPs and DMshave traditionally preferred a centralized architecture.

Figure 12 shows a trend towards decentralization of thestorage of both data products and transactional data. In fact,distributed ledger technologies (DLTs) are increasingly beingused to store transactional or management data related to datatrading. Due to the high cost of storing data in a DLT, it is notyet being considered as a alternative for storing data for sale,except for specific concept models and developing prototypesrelated to healthcare (BurstIQ, MedicalChain), marketing (Datum, already closed) and automotive (AMO), whose feasibilityis yet to be proven.
**4.9** **Can buyers “try” before buying?**
Buyers cannot be sure about the true value of a dataset beforethey get access to it and, e.g., train a ML algorithm and test itsresulting accuracy/performance. This chicken-and-egg problem, known as Arrow’s information (or disclosure) paradox,often deters potential buyers from actually purchasing data. Abig challenge for the data economy is thus to come up withways to reduce the uncertainty for buyer [67]. Next we look athow surveyed entities approach this challenge ( _Challenge 3_ ).

We found that 69% of entities answer this question on theirwebsites, which reflects this is indeed an important issue forthem. They claim to be using one or more of the followingmechanisms: publishing or sending in advance **free samples**of data to potential buyers; allowing **free access** to part of thedata (e.g., some fields of a structured data base); offering a **trial****period** in which to have access to a data feed or subscriptionbased service; providing buyers with a **sandbox** (Battlefin,Otonomo) that lets them experiment with real data beforebidding for it or making a purchase decision; offering a live**demo** of their services and their data; or hosting a **reputation****mechanism** for buyers to rank data and providers.
**4.10** **What security measures are taken?**
_PIMSs_ and DMs often publish high-level information relatedto security of data and data exchanges to gain the trust ofpotential users, be they buyers or sellers. Unsurprisingly, it is_PIMSs_ that express the greatest concern about it: most of theminclude a section dedicated to data security, and address users’and buyers’ frequent concerns in this respect. On the contrary,DPs are generally reluctant to give away any informationabout security, which is considered an internal policy.Some of the measures taken to secure data include: userauthentication and identification; TLS encryption; Anonymization or de-identification of personal information; delivery

through revokable DLT decryption keys or public-key cryptography allowing buyers to decrypt an encrypted data streamor dataset; use of temporary URL to deliver data; secure dataconnectors; tamper-proof data delivery through data signatures and message chaining, which sometimes make use of ablockchain to ensure immutability; or use of specific serviceand software that certifies the origin of data.However, DMs still fail to provide a fully effective solution to avoid unauthorized data replication, and protectingdata ownership was found to be a key challenge ( _Challenge 4_ ).Moving from providing data to providing services has traditionally been the most commonly accepted recipe to mitigatethis risk [65]. For example, ML _niche_ DMs look to sell modeltraining services [20], rather than bulk data to train models as_general-purpose DMs_ do.DMSs and PMPs sell data to be used within their systemsand services, and heavily restrict outgoing data flows. Extending the scope of controlled environments like _embedded_ DMsmight be a means to impose severe barriers to data replicationand enhance the control of the access to data. Still it needs tobe proven whether an open version of such a controlled trustmodel can be scaled and bootstrapped to the entire Internet asstandards like the International Data Spaces [7] and initiativeslike Gaia-X [30] are aiming to.
**5** **RESEARCH ON DATA MARKETPLACES**
Once we have presented the results of the survey, this sectionsummarizes the efforts of the research community in definingmarketplaces, trading and pricing mechanisms for data.Other survey papers have been published regarding DMs [55,64, 66, 67]. Ours is more up-to-date (half of the surveyed entities were founded in 2016 or later), broader in scope, andprovides an in-depth analysis of three times more entities thanprevious works, as shown in Fig. 2. Further - and following ourstudy of 20 of them - this work is also, to the best of our knowledge, the first to address the business models and challengesof personal information management systems ( _PIMSs_ ).

Recent vision papers state the different research challengesenvisaged by the research community when building a DM [29].Not only do they focus on data transactions, but they also emphasize challenges related to discoverability, integration, andtransparency, and deal with the systems perspective, too.An important ongoing research effort is focused on designing marketplaces intended to train ML models. Differentvalue-based data marketplaces have been designed based onthis concept, from sellers selecting a price-value from a mixoffered by the DM [16], to buyers bidding for data and returning a proportional value in return [1], or collaborativeDMs [53]. Some broker prototypes, designed as smart contracts, use cryptography techniques to train - still simple - MLmodels while protecting data privacy [42]. Other prototypesbased on blockchain look to integrate IoT data flows in MLmodels [75]. In such settings, calculating payoffs according tothe accuracy that data brings to a model is a complex problem ( _Challenge 5_ ), often dealt with by approximating Shapleyvalues of data [31, 62]. Some authors have proposed adding adata appraisal layer to select private training data from a MLmarketplace [9, 71].Data pricing has long attracted the attention of the scientificcommunity from different fields [47, 58]. As a result, different schools are applying disparate tools to set arbitrage-freerevenue-maximizing prices to data products, often in specificcontexts. Such tools include auction design [34, 35], differential privacy [32, 45], pricing queries to a database [15, 41] andquality-based pricing [37, 72, 73]. Finally, other authors focuson personal data within the online ad ecosystem [13, 54, 57],spatio-temporal [4, 5], or IoT sensors data [48].Despite the ongoing innovation in the market, most theoretical concepts in research papers regarding pricing, privacypreserving techniques and value-based payment distributionare still under development, and cannot be found in commercial platforms yet. On the contrary, some commercial DMs(IOTA for IoT data, Swash for personal data, or MedicalChainfor health-related data) are implementing blockchain-baseddata exchange platforms, often supported by their own publicwhitepapers. Some of these start-ups, such as the PIMS Datum,have failed due to the exorbitant costs of storing the transacteddata in a blockchain, and most of them restrict the use of DLTsto transactional data.Next, we summarize the open challenges we highlighted inthis paper, and we appoint technologies to address them.**6** **OPEN CHALLENGES**
Despite its remarkable potential and observed initial growth(50% yearly growth of the number of products offered in AWSand DataRade.ai in 2021 [8]), the market for data is still at itsnascent phase. Like all nascent economies, the data economyfaces a yet uncertain future. Regardless of which companiesand business models finally succeed, we identified some key,intertwined, open challenges related to increasing the _practi-__cality_ and _trust_ of the ecosystem:
( _Challenge_ _1_ ) The _current_ _fragmentation_ _of_ _data_ _markets_, asreflected by the ever-growing large number of companies

in market surveys and the variety of data being traded (seeSect. 4.1), makes us think that a consolidation could take placein the future and a new single monopoly or ‘niche’ data tradingchampions may arise. Instead of waiting for such champions tosolve it for everyone, and given the importance and complexityof the task, solutions must be sought that respect transparent Internet and web governance and expansion principles,including openness, standardization, and layering [44].
( _Challenge 2_ ) Regarding data economics, there are open problems and unexplored questions related to pricing, as we pointedout in Sects. 4.3, 4.4, and 5. Moreover, a healthy data marketrequires knowledgeable neutral references (like web servicessuggesting a range of prices to second-hand car sellers basedon the model, year, nº km, etc.) to avoid ending up in a radical and sustained price fluctuation of data products, as recentmeasurement works show [8].
( _Challenge 3_ ) Due to the fact that ‘data’ is an experience good, itis far from obvious for potential buyers to anticipate the valueof data in certain settings such as ML tasks [10, 31]. Hence,developing new solutions allowing buyers to first locate [12]and then select better data, which improve the - still primitive
- ones presented in Sect. 4.9, is important, too [9].
( _Challenge_ _4_ ) Dealing with _ownership_ and fighting againstpiracy and theft of data is of uttermost importance to ensuretrustworthiness in data trading. This task is even more arduouswhen malicious players are able to copy and transmit data atzero cost, and the market lacks a sound notion of authorship,as we pointed out in Sect. 4.10.
( _Challenge 5_ ) Related to _data provenance_, computing _fair com-__pensations_ for providers at scale is an additional challengefor DMs, and especially for PIMSs dealing with individuals(see Sect. 5). In accordance to the research community, suchcompensations must ideally be based on the value they bringto a specific task or buyer, and DMs must be accountable andtransparent about the process [29].
Fortunately, some existing cutting-edge tech will decisivelyhelp in overcoming these daunting challenges, namely:
**Effective data provenance**, even spanning outside of trustecosystems, may be built upon advances on watermarking [2,17, 24, 46], hashing [33, 74], trusted execution [63], and network tomography [14].
**Usage-based** **economics** may be built upon crowdsourcing [39, 40], cryptography [23], and blockchain-based NonFungible Tokens (NFTs) [70].
**Information Centric Network** principles [3] at its data layerprovide a base to handle data naming, routing, and in-networkstorage and replication.
Finally, significant regulatory challenges related to datatrading lie ahead, both for competition authorities and _ex ante_regulatory bodies. Due to their market power, tech companiesare increasingly under the scrutiny of regulators both in the US and the EU. Policymakers are currently evaluating theimposition of some degree of data sharing to dominant techfirms in their effort to balance its market power [11]. However,designing such a policy is complex in the case of data assetsdue to its potential harm to privacy and security.Within the realm of _personal data_, _protecting privacy_ wasthe main purpose of recent legislation in the EU and the US.New legislative proposals in the EU [27, 68] aim to foster datasharing and ‘ _offer an alternative to data handling practice of__major tech platforms_ ’ [28]. Assuming regulatory bodies are ableto enforce such regulations, some authors have proposed thatindividuals are compensated for their personal data [43, 59],while others suggest that entities collecting personal datamust be required to act as a fiduciary [22], or even that themass collection and sharing of sensitive personal data mustbe banned and prosecuted [69].

**7** **CONCLUSION**
We have catalogued ten different business models in this paper,based on a comprehensive survey that analyzed 104 entitiestrading data on the Internet. Through this extensive study,it has become clear to us that most of the challenges theseentities face have to do with _trust_ . On the one hand, sellersexpress an ambition for absolute control of their data, anddemand a strong commitment from marketplaces that data isnot replicated and resold, nor used without their authorization.On the other hand, potential buyers need to test data andknow its value before closing a transaction, and certify thatinformation comes from trusted data sources.Not surprisingly, the most successful market players nowadays are horizontally integrated service providers that protect(rather than share) their most valuable data assets. Traditionalproviders are being challenged by marketplace platforms thatwork both with data sellers and buyers to facilitate data transactions. It is unclear whether there is a one-fits-all solution,and more recent _niche_ coexist with _general-purpose_ DMs inthe market nowadays. Nonetheless, their business model muststill prove feasible, and it is not clear whether specializationis convenient. On the one hand, _niche_ DMs have clear advantages over _general-purpose_ ones. First, because focusing oncertain data space and leveraging their specific expertise letthem provide value-added services both to buyers and sellersalong with data sharing. Second, because their platform isadapted to the kind of data they trade, and they concentratetheir commercial efforts on attracting a specific buyer segment.On the other hand, _niche_ DMs target a much smaller market,and the concept of a one-stop-shop for any kind of data isarguably attractive.Unlike public DMs, _embedded_ DMs and PMPs consider datatrading more as an add-on to the services they already provide.This _commodification_ of data trading has two important competitive advantages. First, they leverage an existing potentialcustomer base on the buy side, which lets them concentrate onfinding the right data partners to attract their captive demand.

Second, they sell data to be used within a specific environment, which significantly reduces the risk of replication andlets them provide more focused, processed, and thereby morevaluable data.Fighting against the data-for-services dynamics of the Internet is the main challenge of _PIMSs_, provided the rights of newdata protection legislation are enforced by competent authorities. They are focusing on gaining the _trust_ of users to build aminimum viable base, yet their feasibility is still to be proven.Consequently, they are struggling to make themselves known,leveraging an increasing concern around privacy on the Internet. Conversely, the variety of existing isolated platformsmay undermine their trustworthiness. A future consolidationmay help them acquire users, though it may well turn the oddsagainst them unless they differentiate themselves from the big‘ _datalords_ ’- why trust your data to a _PIMS_ instead of Internetservice providers? Adopting data _trust_ models might be a wayto overcome this challenge.In conclusion, the data economy is a thriving though controversial ecosystem still under development. A huge corporate,entrepreneurial and research effort aims to _de-silo_ data andenable a healthy trading of such an important asset, which iskey to fully unleash the power of the knowledge economy. Inthis study we have revealed significant differences betweenwhat is working in the market right now and what the market is developing. Through commodifying and specializingdata trading, the market is moving away from horizontallyintegrated monolithic siloed data providers, and towards distributed ‘ _niche_ ’ exchange platforms.

**A** **METHODOLOGY**
The methodology used to carry out the survey consisted ofthe following steps:
(1) **Identification of target companies** trading or making business by delivering data. Companies were identified by either searching the web with relevant keywords, or by browsing through relevant articles andpapers available on the internet.(2) **Making** **a** **quick** **first** **assessment** and classifyingcompanies according to the following basic parameters: type of data they are trading, target industry, typeof clients, and business model.(3) **Formulation of a comprehensive set benchmark****questions** covering all the aspects we want to answerin this study, and defining a preliminary set of possibleanswers to each of them. This was further refinedduring the research process to generate a taxonomyfor presenting the results of the benchmark.(4) **Carrying** **out** **a** **desktop** **research** to dive deeperinto each specific company, answering to the surveyquestions in a data sheet, and generating a detailed information dossier about the company for consultationpurposes in a latter stage as needed.(5) **Building** **the** **data** **taxonomy** **by** **homogenizing****the answers to the benchmark questions** for eachcompany and refining the existing taxonomy of answers that allows the comparison of companies.(6) **Analysis** **of** **the** **results** of this study, both from atechnical and a business perspective, identification ofkey business models and entities operating accordingto them.
Several iterations were needed in order to come up witha comprehensive set of data trading entities, and fully understand the current market situation.
**A.1** **Questions**
Table 3 summarizes the questions considered in the survey,the different answers we found when studying the differententities, and the section where we present the results.In addition to answering the former questions, we gathered some general data to classify each entity, understandits maturity, and measure its popularity. These KPIs includethe foundation year, country of origin, companies backingthe project, the number of employees, how much money theyraised, its AlexaRank and its trend in the last months.
**A.2** **Data collection approach and****limitations**
Data acquisition was the result of a desktop research based onsecondary information available on the internet. As a consequence, the survey relies on information that the target entitiesare directly publishing on their websites, as well as any related material, such as whitepapers, public videos, productbrochures and presentations.

Whenever an answer was not found for any question in thecase of a specific entity, "N/A" (meaning _not available_ ) labelswere used. In general, this situation is due to either a lack of information when analyzing such entities, or due to insufficientdetail of such information to answer the question. We reportthe percentage of entities for which we have information ineach subsection presenting the results of the survey.
